## Post 1, Analogy (orchestra), drives to the guide

Most enterprise AI pilots don't fail because the models are weak.

They fail like an orchestra with brilliant musicians and no conductor.

Every section is world class. The violinists are exceptional. The brass is superb. But there is no score everyone is playing from, no one setting the tempo, and no one accountable for how it all comes together.

So what you get is noise, not music.

That is what I see in a lot of organisations with AI right now.

Great tools. Excited leadership. Impressive demos.

But no operating model, no governance, no single owner, and no real integration into how the business actually runs.

Six months later, the pilot quietly disappears.

Up to 80% of AI initiatives never scale beyond pilot. That is not a technology problem. It is a structure problem.

## Post 2, Customer story (telco), drives to the assessment

A telco I worked with in the region built a genuinely impressive churn prediction model.

The data science team had done brilliant work. The accuracy was there. The demo got a standing ovation in the boardroom.

Then I asked one question: who owns the business outcome this is meant to move, and by how much?

Silence.

Nobody had defined whether success meant fewer cancellations, higher retention revenue, or better save-desk efficiency. So when it came time to fund the rollout, there was no business case to point to.

The model still sits in a dashboard nobody acts on.

This is the pattern I see again and again. The technology works. The accountability doesn't exist.

## Post 3, Customer story (utilities, governance), drives to services

A common belief I hear: "governance will slow our AI down."

A utilities company in the Gulf proved the opposite.

Before they deployed a single production model, they built the governance first. Data provenance, explainability, clear accountability, the lot. It felt slow at the time. Their data team was not thrilled.

Then their first AI application, a demand forecasting model for load balancing, went live. It sailed through regulatory review in three weeks.

A competitor running a comparable model with no governance documentation spent seven months going back and forth with the same regulator.

By the time the competitor was approved, the first company had already captured most of the opportunity.

Governance is not the brakes. In a tightening regulatory environment like the UAE, it is the thing that lets you move with confidence while others are stuck in review cycles.

## Posting tips

- **Cadence:** 3 posts a week is plenty. Space them out, do not dump all six at once.
- **Best times (UAE):** Sun to Thu, around 8 to 9am or 1pm GST.
- **First comment:** drop the link in the first comment instead of the body if you want maximum reach (LinkedIn slightly favours posts without outbound links in the body). Test both.
- **Reply to every comment** in the first hour. It is the single biggest driver of reach.
- **Reuse:** each of your six articles can become 3 to 4 more posts. This bank is a starting point, not the whole well.
